## What is a Warband?

A warband is your gang of fighters — the core of your Mordheim experience. You recruit, equip, and lead them through a series of linked games called a campaign, hunting for wyrdstone in the ruins and clashing with rival warbands along the way. Between games, your warriors gain experience, develop new skills, and suffer lasting injuries. Warbands grow, evolve, and tell their own stories — no two campaigns play out quite the same.

___

## Choosing a Warband

Each warband has a unique roster, equipment list, and special rules that define its playstyle. Before you start, you'll want to pick one that suits how you want to play — aggressive or cautious, range-heavy or martial, elite and expensive or numerous and cheap.

Warbands are recruited from a starting budget of **500 gold crowns**. You spend this to hire fighters and purchase their starting equipment. A warband must include a minimum of three models, and most have an upper limit on total size — typically around 12–15 warriors.

## Types of Warbands

The available warbands span a wide range of races and factions drawn from the Warhammer world. Warbands are organised by grade, reflecting the quality and play-tested nature of their rulesets. They are available on [broheim.net](https://broheim.net/warbands.html), each listed under their respective grade.

### Grade 1A — Official GW (Mordheim Rulebook & core Town Cryer)

The original warbands — the most thoroughly tested and the backbone of the game.

- **Mercenaries** (Reikland, Middenheim, and Marienburg) — human sell-swords, versatile and well-equipped
- **Sisters of Sigmar** — zealous warrior-priestesses of the Empire's faith
- **Cult of the Possessed** — corrupted humans and daemons serving the Shadowlord
- **Skaven** — ratmen from the underworld, swarming and unpredictable
- **Undead** — vampires, zombies, and risen warriors under a necromancer's command
- **Witch Hunters** — puritan warriors of the Empire, dedicated to destroying evil in all its forms
- **Beastmen Raiders** — savage creatures of the dark forests
- **Averlanders** — tough, self-reliant hill-folk from the mountain passes

### Grade 1B — Unofficial GW (later Town Cryer & supplements)

A broad expansion of the roster with warbands also published by Games Workshop, including Dark Elves, Dwarf Rangers, Lizardmen, Bretonnian Knights, Norse Explorers, Shadow Warriors, Amazons, Forest Goblins, Pirates, and many more.

### Grade 1C — Community supplements

Experimental warbands from community supplements, usually of high quality and well play-tested — often endorsed by Games Workshop staff. Best explored once you are comfortable with the core game.

### Grade 2 & 3

Further experimental warbands, some community play-tested, some still in draft stages.

## Warband Structure

All warbands follow the same basic structure, dividing fighters into three roles.

### The Leader

The heart of your warband — you have exactly one, and they are always your most capable fighter. Leaders provide a morale bonus to nearby warriors and tend to have the widest access to skills and equipment. If your leader falls out of action and suffers a serious injury, it can have lasting consequences for your warband's campaign performance.

### Heroes

Your named characters — the lieutenants, champions, and specialists who give your warband its character. Each warband has a limit on how many heroes it can field (typically four to six in total, including the leader). Heroes gain experience individually, develop unique skills, and can suffer serious injuries that are tracked from game to game.

### Henchmen

Your rank-and-file fighters. They are cheaper to recruit and field in groups — all models in a henchman group advance together, sharing experience and gaining the same skills. Henchmen cannot hold named equipment or develop the same breadth of skills as heroes, but they make up the bulk of your warband's numbers and firepower.

---

## Equipment

Warriors are equipped from their warband's specific equipment list, which defines what weapons, armour, and other items they may purchase. Heroes typically have access to a wider range, and can visit the Trading Post between games to seek out rarer items. Henchmen are generally limited to a narrower selection.

Equipment choices matter: weapons have different strengths and special rules, and armour provides saves in combat. Building a coherent fighting force — balancing close combat, ranged attacks, and resilience — is a key part of warband strategy.


## Grades

Warbands are organised by grade, reflecting the quality and play-tested nature of their rulesets.

They are available on [broheim.net](https://broheim.net/warbands.html), each listed under their respective grade.

- **Grade 1A** — The original warbands from the Mordheim Rulebook, and core Town Cryer issues. Official rules published by Games Workshop.
- **Grade 1B** — Unofficial warbands from later Town Cryer issues and supplements published by Games Workshop.
- **Grade 1C** — Experimental warbands released through community supplements, usually of high-quality and have been play-tested. Often endorsed by Games Workshop staff. 
- **Grade 2 & 3** — Further experimental warbands, some community play-tested, some in draft stages.
  <br>
  
> [!tip] New to Mordheim? 
> Start with a Grade 1A or 1B warband. These have the most complete and well-tested rulesets, making them the easiest to learn and the most reliably balanced against other warbands. Grade 1C and beyond can be great fun, but are best appreciated once you have a campaign or two under your belt.
